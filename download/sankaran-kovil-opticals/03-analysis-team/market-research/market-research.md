## 1. Indian Eyewear Market Overview

### Market Size and Valuation

The Indian eyewear market is one of the fastest-growing consumer segments globally, driven by rising disposable incomes, increasing digital screen time, growing awareness of eye health, and the country's large and young population. The market is experiencing a multi-billion-dollar transformation that presents substantial opportunities for both organized and independent retailers.

According to Grand View Research, the India spectacles market was estimated at **USD 4.1 billion (approximately INR 34,300 crore) in 2024** and is expected to grow at a **CAGR of 11.4% from 2025 to 2030**. Coherent Market Insights projects the India spectacles market to reach **USD 4.80 billion in 2026** and grow to **USD 10.37 billion by 2033**, exhibiting a CAGR of 11.6% during the forecast period. IMARC Group estimates the India spectacles market reached **USD 4,273.71 million in 2025**, projected to reach **USD 7,913.27 million by 2034** at a CAGR of approximately 7.1%.

The broader Indian eyewear market (including sunglasses, contact lenses, and accessories) is valued at approximately **USD 6.6 billion (INR 55,000+ crore) in 2024**, according to Custom Market Insights, with projections to reach **USD 11.93 billion by 2033**. Maximize Market Research estimates the overall India eyewear market to reach nearly **USD 13.58 billion by 2030** at a CAGR of **11.90%** during the forecast period. India's luxury eyewear segment was valued at **USD 891 million in 2024** and is projected to reach **USD 1,721 million by 2030**, growing at a CAGR of approximately 11.72% (MarkNtel Advisors).

On a global scale, the eyewear market was valued at **USD 200.46 billion in 2024** (Grand View Research) and is expected to grow at a CAGR of **8.6% from 2025 to 2030**. Fortune Business Insights projects the global market at **USD 181.75 billion in 2025**, growing to **USD 330.08 billion by 2034**. India's eyewear market, at ~11-12% CAGR, is expanding at approximately **1.5x the rate of the overall Indian retail market** (Lenskart IPO Industry Report).

### Key Market Trends for 2025-2026

- **Prescription Glasses Dominance:** The prescription glasses segment accounts for **over 69% of global eyewear revenue** (Grand View Research, 2024). In India, spectacles and sunglasses together command **72.14% of the market share** (Future Market Insights).
- **Online Channel Growth:** Online retail contributed the highest market share of **53.4% in 2025** in the India spectacles market (Coherent Market Insights), driven by inherent convenience and competitive pricing. However, the overall online penetration in Indian eyewear remains only **3-5% of total market sales**, with significant headroom for growth compared to mature markets like the US (15-20% penetration).
- **Organized Sector Expansion:** The organized eyewear sector accounts for approximately **40-45% of the market by value**, while the unorganized/independent sector still dominates in volume terms with **50-60% of transactions**. Major chains like Lenskart (2,723 stores), Titan Eye+ (905 stores), and Specsmakers (275+ stores) are aggressively expanding into Tier 2 and Tier 3 cities.
- **Lenskart's IPO and Scale:** Lenskart, India's largest eyewear retailer, filed for IPO in October 2025 targeting **USD 8-10 billion valuation**. - **Blue-Light Blocking Growth:** The blue-light blocking glasses segment is experiencing rapid growth, with the global market projected to grow from **USD 2.9 billion in 2024 to USD 5.8 billion by 2034** at a CAGR of 7.3% (Allied Market Research). - **Myopia Epidemic:** With **23% of Indian schoolchildren now myopic** (BW Healthcare World), the demand for prescription eyewear among younger populations is surging, creating a long-term structural demand driver.

## 5. Demand Drivers

### Healthcare and Demographic Factors

**Rising Myopia Prevalence:** The single most powerful demand driver for the Indian eyewear market is the rapid increase in myopia (nearsightedness), particularly among children and young adults. With 23% of Indian schoolchildren now myopic (BW Healthcare World), up from approximately 13% a decade ago, the addressable population for prescription eyewear is expanding dramatically. In urban areas, myopia prevalence among children reaches **8.5%** versus **6.1% in rural areas** (Dr. Zamindar's Eye Centre). The Myopia Institute projects that nearly **5 billion people globally will be affected by myopia by 2050**, creating a multi-decade structural growth driver. For the Sankarankovil market, this translates to a growing base of young customers who need regular prescription updates, with an average replacement cycle of 12-18 months.

**Aging Population and Presbyopia:** While Tamil Nadu has a lower population growth rate than most Indian states, its aging population creates sustained demand for reading glasses, bifocal, and progressive lenses. Presbyopia typically begins at age 40-45 and affects virtually 100% of the population by age 55-60. With approximately 25-30% of the catchment population in the 36-55 age group, presbyopia correction represents a major and growing demand driver.

**Diabetes-Related Eye Conditions:** India has approximately 101 million people with diabetes (IDF 2024), and diabetic retinopathy affects a significant percentage of these patients. In Tamil Nadu, diabetes prevalence is among the highest in India, driving demand for specialized eyewear and regular eye examinations.

### Lifestyle and Behavioral Factors

**Digital Screen Time:** The proliferation of smartphones, laptops, and tablets has dramatically increased screen time across all age groups. A systematic review published in JAMA Network Open confirmed a direct dose-response association between digital screen time and myopia risk. Even without a prescription, the awareness of digital eye strain has driven demand for blue-light blocking glasses, which saw the India market grow from USD 96.51 million toward a projected USD 358.82 million at 11.69% CAGR (Cognitive Market Research). For the Sankarankovil catchment, where smartphone penetration is growing rapidly and online education is increasingly common, screen-related eye strain is an emerging demand driver.

**Fashion and Self-Expression:** Eyewear has evolved from a purely functional medical device to a fashion accessory and statement of personal style. Social media platforms (Instagram, YouTube) have accelerated this trend, with influencers and celebrities making specific frame styles trendy. This "fashion-ification" of eyewear has expanded the addressable market beyond those with vision correction needs to include individuals who purchase non-prescription frames and sunglasses purely for style.

**Increasing Disposable Income:** India's middle class is expanding, and even in semi-urban areas like Sankarankovil, household incomes are rising. The NRI population's contribution to local spending also creates seasonal spikes in premium eyewear demand during holiday visit periods.

### Awareness and Accessibility Factors

**Government Health Programs:** Tamil Nadu's government health programs, including the Chief Minister's Comprehensive Health Insurance Scheme and school vision screening programs, have increased awareness of eye health and the importance of regular eye check-ups. While government schemes may provide free spectacles, they also create awareness that drives private purchases for those who can afford them.

**Hospital Eye Check-Up Culture:** The presence of Aravind Eye Hospital and other eye care facilities in the broader Tenkasi-Tirunelveli region means a significant portion of the population receives regular eye examinations. Patients who receive prescriptions from hospitals often seek local shops for dispensing, creating a pipeline of informed customers.

**Digital Awareness:** Even in semi-urban areas, internet and social media are creating awareness about eye health, the latest eyewear trends, and available products. Customers increasingly research online before purchasing, leading to more informed and demanding consumers.

## 8. Regulatory Environment

### Business Registration and Licensing Requirements

Operating an optical retail shop in Tamil Nadu requires compliance with several regulatory frameworks at both the central and state levels:

**MD-42 Registration (Mandatory):** Under the Medical Device Rules, 2017 (MDR 2017) administered by the Central Drugs Standard Control Organization (CDSCO), all optical retail businesses dealing in spectacles and contact lenses must obtain **MD-42 registration**. This is a mandatory license for manufacturing or importing medical devices. As an optical retailer, the shop must purchase medical devices (spectacle lenses, contact lenses) only from MD-42 registered manufacturers or importers. The CDSCO has been strengthening enforcement of these regulations, with a transition deadline of **May 30, 2026** for all registrations to be migrated from drug to medical device classification. Non-compliance can result in penalties including closure of business.

**Trade License:** A trade license from the Sankarankovil Municipality (or local panchayat) is required for operating any commercial establishment. This is a standard municipal requirement.

**GST Registration:** Any business with annual turnover exceeding INR 20 lakh (INR 10 lakh for special category states) must register for GST. For an optical shop, GST registration is essential for purchasing stock from wholesale suppliers and claiming input tax credits.

**Shop and Establishment Registration:** Under the Tamil Nadu Shops and Establishments Act, all shops must register with the local labor department, complying with regulations on working hours, employee welfare, and safety standards.

**Professional Registration:** If the shop employs an optometrist to perform eye testing, the optometrist must be registered with the Tamil Nadu Optometrists and Opticians Council (or relevant state regulatory body). Eye testing by unregistered practitioners is illegal in many states.

### GST Rate Structure for Optical Products (2025 Update)

The GST 2.0 reforms effective **September 22, 2025**, have significantly changed the GST landscape for optical retailers. India's GST has been simplified to a **two-slab structure (5% and 18%)**:

| Product Category | GST Rate (Pre-Sep 2025) | GST Rate (Post-Sep 2025) | Impact |
|-----------------|------------------------|-------------------------|--------|
| Spectacle Lenses (Prescription) | 12% | **5%** | Significant relief - reduces cost to consumer |
| Frames (Spectacle) | 12-18% | **5%** | Major reduction - frames now at lowest slab |
| Contact Lenses | 12-18% | **5%** | Significant benefit for contact lens retailers |
| Sunglasses | 18-28% | **18%** | Simplified from variable rates to single 18% rate |
| Metal Frames | 18% | **5%** | Major reduction from 18% to 5% |

**Implications for Sankaran Kovil Opticals:**
- The reduction of GST on prescription eyewear products from 12-18% to 5% is a significant positive development. It reduces the final cost to consumers by approximately 7-13%, potentially increasing demand.
- The price reduction may also help local optical shops compete more effectively with online retailers, as the tax advantage narrows the price gap.
- Input tax credit management becomes simpler with the two-slab structure.
- Sunglasses at 18% remain in the higher slab, but the removal of the previous 28% rate for premium sunglasses is beneficial.

### Medical Device Classification

Spectacle lenses and contact lenses are classified as **medical devices** under the MDR 2017. Key implications:
- Prescription spectacle lenses are typically classified as **Class A or Class B** (low to moderate risk).
- Contact lenses are classified as **Class B or Class C** depending on the type (daily wear vs. extended wear).
- Importers and manufacturers must have valid MD-42 licenses.
- Retailers must maintain records of purchase from licensed entities only.
- Post-market surveillance requirements are being strengthened under the 2025 regulatory updates.

### Consumer Protection and Compliance

- **Consumer Protection Act, 2019:** Optical shops must comply with product quality standards, accurate pricing display, and refund/return policies.
- **Legal Metrology Act:** All products must carry MRP (Maximum Retail Price) labels, and billing must be done at MRP or below.
- **Weights and Measures:** Prescription lenses must be dispensed as per the prescribed specifications, with proper documentation.

## 9. Technology Disruption Impact

### Online Retailers: The Lenskart Effect

The most significant technological disruption in Indian eyewear retail has come from Lenskart, which has fundamentally changed how Indians purchase eyewear. Key metrics that illustrate the scale of disruption:

- **Revenue:** Rs.6,653 crore in FY25 (up 22.5% YoY), with **17 million glasses delivered in 9 months**
- **Store Network:** 2,723 stores globally (2,067 in India + 656 overseas), targeting **4,000 stores by 2030**
- **Valuation:** USD 8-10 billion IPO valuation (October 2025), market cap crossing **Rs.70,000 crore**
- **Market Share:** Approximately **25% of the organized eyewear market** and **4-6% of India's total prescription eyewear sector**
- **Revenue Mix:** ~60% of Indian revenue from online/digital channels, ~40% from physical stores
- **Technology:** AI-powered eye testing (Lenskart Blu), 3D face scanning, robotic manufacturing accurate to 3 decimal places, virtual try-on

**Impact on Local Shops like Sankaran Kovil Opticals:**
- **Price Benchmarking:** Lenskart's starting prices of Rs.399-599 for complete eyeglasses have redefined consumer price expectations. Even customers who prefer local shops now use Lenskart prices as negotiation benchmarks, compressing local shop margins.
- **Youth Migration:** First-time glasses wearers and young adults (18-30 years) are increasingly drawn to Lenskart's trendy designs, easy online interface, and home delivery.
- **Home Try-On Service:** Lenskart's home try-on feature (where they deliver 5 frames to your home for trial) has eroded the key advantage of physical stores—being able to try frames before purchasing.
- **Brand Awareness:** Massive national advertising (IPL sponsorships, TV campaigns, celebrity endorsements by Katrina Kaif, Bhuvan Bam) has created top-of-mind brand recall even in semi-urban Sankarankovil.

However, Lenskart's **market share of <8% of India's total prescription eyewear** means the vast majority of the market remains with local and independent shops. The key for Sankaran Kovil Opticals is to compete on service, personalization, and trust rather than attempting to match Lenskart's prices.

### AI-Powered Eye Testing

Lenskart's "Lenskart Blu" technology and similar AI-powered eye testing solutions are democratizing access to vision testing. These systems use smartphone-based autorefraction to generate preliminary prescriptions without the need for a trained optometrist. While these AI-generated prescriptions are not yet considered a substitute for a comprehensive eye examination by a qualified professional, they are driving awareness of refractive errors and creating a pipeline of informed customers who need eyewear.

For Sankaran Kovil Opticals, investing in a modern autorefractometer and computerized eye testing equipment (approximately INR 50,000-2,00,000) can provide a credible alternative to AI-only testing, offering the added value of professional interpretation and comprehensive eye health assessment.

### Teleophthalmology and Remote Consultations

Teleophthalmology—the remote diagnosis and management of eye conditions using telecommunications technology—is an emerging trend in India, particularly relevant for rural and semi-urban areas where access to ophthalmologists is limited. Aravind Eye Care System has been a pioneer in teleophthalmology, using it to extend their reach to remote villages.

**Implications for Local Optical Retail:**
- Teleophthalmology may increase the number of people receiving eye prescriptions remotely, who then need local shops for dispensing.
- Partnerships with teleophthalmology providers could position Sankaran Kovil Opticals as a dispensing partner, creating a referral pipeline.
- However, teleophthalmology services that include eyewear dispensing (direct-to-patient delivery) could also bypass local shops entirely.

### E-Commerce Platforms (Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra)

Beyond dedicated eyewear retailers, major e-commerce platforms serve as significant indirect competitors:
- **Amazon.in and Flipkart** host thousands of eyewear sellers offering frames and complete spectacles at competitive prices, reaching every corner of India through their delivery networks, including rural Tamil Nadu.
- **Quick Commerce** (Blinkit, Zepto, BigBasket) is beginning to offer eyewear products for rapid delivery in urban areas. Titan Eye+ is exploring quick commerce partnerships.
- The **digital transformation of traditional retail in Tenkasi district** (as documented in academic research) means that local consumers are increasingly comfortable with online shopping, creating a gradual shift away from purely physical retail.

### Counter-Strategy: Technology Adoption by Local Shops

To counter technology disruption, local optical shops can adopt selected technologies:
- **Digital Catalogues and Point-of-Sale (POS):** Modern POS systems with digital frame catalogues expand perceived inventory without requiring physical stock.
- **Online Presence:** Google Business Profile, WhatsApp Business, and basic social media presence help local shops compete for digital visibility.
- **Digital Marketing:** Targeted local digital advertising (Google Ads, Facebook/Instagram Ads geotargeted to Sankarankovil) can capture consumers searching for eyewear online.
- **Automated Lens Edging:** In-house automated lens cutting equipment (approximately INR 1-3 lakh) enables faster service and better margins on lens dispensing.
