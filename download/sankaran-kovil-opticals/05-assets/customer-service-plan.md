## 1. Customer Service Standards & Policies

### 1.1 Core Service Philosophy

Sankaran Kovil Opticals is committed to delivering an exceptional eye care and eyewear shopping experience that reflects the warmth, trust, and community spirit of Sankarankovil. Our service philosophy is built on three foundational pillars: **Clinical Excellence**, **Personalised Care**, and **Community Trust**. Every interaction a customer has with our store — from the moment they walk in to their follow-up visit six months later — must embody these pillars. We recognise that purchasing eyewear is not merely a transactional act; it is a health decision and a personal style statement, often made with the involvement of family members. Our service standards must reflect this understanding.

We adopt a "customer-first" approach in every decision, ensuring that policies are designed to empower staff to resolve issues on the spot rather than escalate unnecessarily. Research in optical retail consistently shows that practices which invest in customer experience see revenue growth of 4–8% above market average (20/20 Magazine). Our goal is to become the most trusted and preferred optical destination in Sankarankovil and the surrounding taluks of Tirunelveli district.

### 1.2 Service Level Commitments

| Service Metric | Standard | Measurement |
|---|---|---|
| Greeting time (customer to first staff contact) | Within 30 seconds of entry | Mystery shopper audits |
| Maximum waiting time for eye test | 15 minutes (appointment); 30 minutes (walk-in) | Appointment log review |
| Eye test duration | Minimum 20 minutes per comprehensive test | Optometrist sign-off |
| Frame selection assistance | Within 2 minutes of customer approaching display | Observation audit |
| Billing/checkout time | Maximum 5 minutes per transaction | POS timestamp audit |
| Complaint resolution (Level 1) | Within 15 minutes | Complaint register |
| Complaint resolution (Level 2) | Within 48 hours | Complaint register |
| Callback/follow-up after purchase | Within 7 days | CRM follow-up log |
| Frame adjustment service | Within 10 minutes (walk-in) | Service log |

### 1.3 Service Policies Overview

- **No-Obligation Eye Test**: Every customer, whether they purchase from us or not, is entitled to a free basic vision screening. This builds goodwill and positions us as a community health resource.
- **Price Transparency**: All frame and lens prices must be clearly displayed. No hidden charges. Staff must explain the full cost breakdown (frame + lens + coating + taxes) before the customer confirms the order.
- **Prescription Privacy**: All customer prescriptions and personal health data are stored securely and shared only with the customer and authorised staff. In compliance with Indian data protection norms, we maintain strict confidentiality.
- **Children & Elderly Priority**: Customers above 60 years and children below 12 years receive priority service and dedicated attention.
- **Multilingual Service**: Staff must be conversant in Tamil and functional in English. Where possible, we will have team members who can assist in Malayalam for customers from nearby Kerala-border areas.
- **Sunday & Festival Hours**: Recognising that many working customers can only visit on weekends, the store will remain open on Sundays (with reduced hours) and during key festival seasons (Deepavali, Pongal, Ayudha Puja).

### 1.4 Ethical Selling Policy

We enforce a strict ethical selling policy: staff must never upsell a customer into a product that does not genuinely serve their needs. Lens recommendations must be based on the prescription, lifestyle, and budget of the customer — not on commission targets. If a customer's prescription does not warrant an expensive progressive lens, staff must recommend the appropriate alternative. This builds long-term trust, which is the cornerstone of our community-based business model in a tier-3 town like Sankarankovil.

## 2. In-Store Experience Design

### 2.1 Waiting Area Comfort

The waiting area is the customer's first physical touchpoint after entering the store. For an optical shop in a semi-urban Tamil Nadu market, many customers visit with family — often 2–3 members accompanying the patient. The waiting area must accommodate families comfortably and reduce the perceived wait time.

**Seating Arrangement**: Provide a minimum of 6–8 chairs in the waiting zone, arranged in a semi-circular configuration facing the eye test room entrance. Use cushioned chairs with armrests (elderly-friendly). Include a small bench or two-seater for families with children. The seating material should be easy to clean and maintain in the local climate.

**Reading & Engagement Material**: Place a selection of Tamil and English magazines, newspapers (Dinamalar, Dinathanthi, The Hindu), and educational eye care brochures on a low table. Include a framed display of "Did You Know?" eye care facts in Tamil — for example, the importance of annual eye checks for diabetics (given Tamil Nadu's high diabetes prevalence).

**Refreshments**: Offer complimentary drinking water (a filtered water dispenser is standard). During peak festival seasons, offer filter coffee or tea as a gesture of Tamil hospitality. This small touch significantly enhances the emotional connection customers feel with the store.

**Ambient Environment**: Maintain comfortable room temperature (24–26°C) using ceiling fans and, if budget permits, an air conditioner. Play soft instrumental or light Tamil film music at low volume. Ensure the area is well-ventilated and free from chemical odours (from lens cleaning solutions or frame polishing).

**Digital Integration**: Mount a small LED screen in the waiting area displaying eye health tips, new frame arrivals, and promotional offers on a rotating loop. The content should be in Tamil with English subtitles, and the screen should not be intrusive or loud.

### 2.2 Frame Display Organization

Frame displays are the centrepiece of the retail experience. Effective visual merchandising can increase frame sales by 20–30% (AA Optical Co.). Our display strategy balances aesthetics with functionality:

**Primary Display Wall — Organised by Category**: The main display wall (facing the entrance or the customer's natural sightline) should showcase frames organised by clear categories. We recommend the following sections:
- **Men's Collection** (left side): Organised by style — Full Rim, Half Rim, Rimless, Rectangle, Round, Wayfarer.
- **Women's Collection** (centre): Organised by style and colour — Cat Eye, Oversized, Geometric, Floral, Transparent.
- **Kids' Collection** (lower shelves, easily accessible): Bright colours, durable frames, character-themed options.
- **Premium / Designer Section** (dedicated glass case or illuminated shelf): Higher-priced frames from trusted brands.
- **Budget-Friendly Section** (clearly marked): Quality frames under ₹500 for price-sensitive customers.

**Display Height & Accessibility**: Place the best-selling and newest frames at eye level (135–150 cm from floor). Reserve top shelves for overstock and less popular styles. Ensure lower shelves are easily accessible for children and elderly customers who may need to sit while browsing.

**Pricing Tags**: Every frame must have a clear, neatly printed price tag visible to the customer. Tags should include: MRP (inclusive of GST), frame material (acetate, metal, TR90), and size (mm). For lens-inclusive combo offers, place a combo price card on the display shelf.

**Rotation Schedule**: Rotate frame displays every 4–6 weeks to maintain visual freshness. Seasonal displays (e.g., sunglasses for summer, anti-glare computer glasses during exam season) should be prominently featured at the entrance or on a dedicated feature table.

**Mirror Placement**: Install full-length mirrors at multiple locations near the frame displays, angled to reflect natural light. Include at least one "selfie mirror" area with good lighting, as younger customers frequently want to photograph themselves trying on frames.

### 2.3 Trial Room Setup

A dedicated frame trial area (sometimes called a "try-on station") is essential for customers to evaluate frames in comfort and privacy. Unlike large metro stores, many small-town optical shops lack this feature, which gives us a competitive advantage.

**Physical Setup**: Designate a well-lit corner of the store with a full-length mirror (minimum 5 feet tall) mounted on a stable stand. The floor beneath should be a contrasting tile colour to help customers see dropped items. Place a small side table nearby for customers to rest their personal belongings (bags, mobile phones, existing spectacles).

**Lighting**: Install warm-white LED downlights (3000–3500K) above the trial mirror to simulate natural daylight. Avoid harsh fluorescent lighting, which distorts frame colours and creates unflattering reflections. The goal is for customers to see themselves as they would appear in natural light outdoors — the real-world condition in which they will wear their spectacles.

**Handheld Mirror**: Provide a high-quality handheld mirror at the trial station for customers who prefer close-up inspection of frame fit on the bridge and temples.

**Privacy Considerations**: While the trial area does not need to be a fully enclosed room, it should be positioned away from high-traffic zones. Customers should feel comfortable trying on multiple frames without feeling observed by other waiting customers. A decorative screen or a well-placed display rack can create a semi-private zone.

**Assistance Protocol**: A staff member should be stationed nearby (but not hovering) to assist with frame selection, provide additional mirrors, and offer opinions when asked. The staff member should carry a tray with lens cleaning wipes and tissues.

### 2.4 Lighting Recommendations

Lighting is the single most critical element of an optical store's atmosphere. According to 20/20 Magazine and GoAnagram's optical design research, effective lighting can increase eyewear sales by highlighting key products and creating a premium ambience. For Sankarankovil's climate and infrastructure conditions, we recommend a layered lighting approach:

**Ambient Lighting (General Illumination)**: Use 4000K cool-white LED panel lights on the ceiling for overall store brightness. This provides a clean, professional look without being clinical. Target 300–400 lux in general areas and 500–600 lux near the frame displays.

**Accent Lighting (Frame Highlights)**: Install adjustable LED spotlights (3500K warm-white) directed at premium and featured frame collections. Each spotlight should be aimed at a 15–30 degree angle to create depth and minimise glare on the frame lenses. This "jewellery store" lighting effect makes frames appear more premium and draws the customer's eye.

**Eye Test Room Lighting**: The refraction room requires specialized lighting. Use dimmable ceiling lights (controlled by the optometrist), a well-positioned Snellen chart projector/screen with its own backlighting, and a dedicated near-vision task light for reading tests. The room should allow the optometrist to easily switch between well-lit and dim conditions for pupil response and retinoscopy tests.

**Mirror & Trial Area Lighting**: As mentioned in the trial room section, warm-white LEDs (3000–3500K) simulating daylight colour temperature. Avoid blue-white lighting that makes skin tones appear washed out and frame colours look inaccurate.

**Window & Natural Light Management**: Sankarankovil receives intense tropical sunlight. Use sheer curtains or UV-filtering window films to allow natural light while preventing glare on display cases. Avoid placing frame displays directly opposite windows where sunlight creates reflections.

**Power Backup**: Given the frequent power fluctuations in semi-urban Tamil Nadu, the store must have an inverter/UPS system capable of maintaining full lighting for at least 2 hours. Eye tests cannot be conducted in poor lighting, and dim stores drive away walk-in customers.

## 3. Staff Behavior Guidelines

### 3.1 Professional Appearance

Staff are the face of Sankaran Kovil Opticals. In a small town where personal relationships and word-of-mouth are the primary drivers of business, the appearance and demeanour of every team member directly impacts the store's reputation. All staff must adhere to the following appearance standards:

- **Uniform**: A branded polo shirt or kurta in the store's primary colour (to be defined) with the shop logo embroidered on the chest. Uniforms must be clean, neatly ironed, and worn every working day.
- **Identification**: All customer-facing staff must wear a name badge with their first name and designation (e.g., "Kumar — Optometrist" or "Priya — Frame Consultant"). This makes interactions more personal and builds accountability.
- **Grooming**: Clean and well-groomed appearance at all times. Nails must be trimmed (important for handling frames and lenses). Hair should be tidy. Use of mild perfume is acceptable; strong fragrances are not (they can irritate customers and the optometrist's workspace).
- **Footwear**: Clean, closed-toe footwear (professional and safe for a retail environment). Avoid sandals or chappals on the shop floor.
- **Accessories**: Staff who wear spectacles should ensure they are clean and well-fitted — they are, after all, walking advertisements for the shop's products. If staff wear contact lenses, this should not be visible to customers.

### 3.2 Communication Standards

Effective communication is the backbone of excellent customer service in an optical retail environment. Our staff must communicate with clarity, empathy, and professionalism at all times:

- **Language**: Tamil is the primary language of communication. All staff must be fluent in spoken Tamil. English proficiency is desirable for serving customers from other states, NRIs visiting family in the region, and for understanding technical lens specifications. Staff should switch languages naturally based on the customer's preference without making any customer feel uncomfortable.
- **Tone & Volume**: Speak in a warm, moderate tone. Avoid shouting across the store — walk to the customer to assist them. Maintain a respectful "neenga" or respectful form of address, especially with elderly customers.
- **Active Listening**: When a customer describes their vision problem or frame preference, the staff member must listen fully without interrupting. Use verbal acknowledgements ("Sari, sari", "Kandippa") and nod to show engagement. Paraphrase the customer's needs back to them before making recommendations.
- **Avoid Jargon**: Optical terminology (astigmatism, presbyopia, index, abbe value) must be explained in simple Tamil. For example, instead of saying "You need anti-reflective coating with blue light filter," explain: "This coating reduces glare from screens and headlights, which reduces eye strain — especially useful if you use a mobile phone or computer frequently."
- **Body Language**: Maintain open body language — uncrossed arms, eye contact, and a genuine smile. Lean slightly forward when a customer is speaking. Avoid checking your mobile phone or attending to other tasks while a customer is talking.
- **No Hard Selling**: Staff must never pressure a customer to make a purchase. Use suggestive language ("This frame suits your face shape very well") rather than pushy language ("You should buy this one today only"). The customer must always feel in control of their decision.

### 3.3 Teamwork & Internal Communication

- Staff must support each other during peak hours. If one team member is with a customer and another customer needs help, any available staff member should step in — not wait for a specific person.
- Shift handovers must include communication about any pending customer requests, frames on order, or unresolved issues.
- Monthly team meetings to discuss customer feedback, share success stories, and address recurring service issues.

### 3.4 Personal Device Policy

Staff personal mobile phones must be kept on silent mode and stored in the back office or locker area during working hours. Personal calls should be taken only during designated breaks. In case of an emergency, family members can call the store's landline. This policy ensures customers receive undivided attention and reinforces a professional atmosphere.

## 4. Customer Greeting & Assistance Protocol

### 4.1 The First Impression — Entry Greeting

Research from the optical retail industry consistently identifies the first 30 seconds of a customer's store visit as the most critical moment in shaping their overall experience (LinkedIn: Great Customer Service in the Optical Industry). At Sankaran Kovil Opticals, we implement a structured greeting protocol:

**Within 10 seconds of entry**: Every customer who enters the store must be acknowledged with a warm smile and a verbal greeting. Even if all staff are busy, a simple "Vanakkam! Welcome, please be seated — we'll be with you shortly" makes the customer feel recognised and valued.

**Within 30 seconds**: A staff member must approach the customer, make eye contact, and offer assistance. The standard opening line should be open-ended: "How can I help you today?" (Tamil: "Innaikku enna seyya veenum?"). This invites the customer to explain their need rather than being funnelled into a specific service immediately.

**Do Not Judge by Appearance**: Every customer — whether they appear to be a high-value purchaser or a budget-conscious student — must receive the same level of attention and respect. In a small town, the person who buys a simple ₹500 reading glass today may recommend the shop to ten others, including high-value customers.

### 4.2 Needs Assessment

Before leading a customer to any section of the store, staff must conduct a brief needs assessment:

1. **"Is this your first visit to our shop?"** — Helps tailor the interaction. First-time visitors get a brief store tour; returning customers are greeted by name (if recognised) or asked about their previous purchase.
2. **"Are you here for an eye test, to buy spectacles, for a repair, or just browsing?"** — Immediately categorises the customer's need and routes them appropriately.
3. **"Do you have an existing prescription?"** — If yes, collect it immediately and review it. If no, guide them to the eye test process.
4. **"What type of lenses do you currently use?"** — Identifies whether the customer is due for an upgrade or has specific lens preferences.
5. **"Any particular style or colour preference?"** — Opens the frame selection conversation.

This 60-second assessment dramatically improves the efficiency of the customer's visit and makes them feel understood.

### 4.3 Active Browsing vs. Directed Assistance

Not all customers want immediate assistance. Some prefer to browse independently. Staff must be trained to read customer cues:
- **Customer picks up a frame and examines it closely**: Approach and offer to provide more information about that frame.
- **Customer seems uncertain or is looking around**: Proactively offer to guide them through the collection.
- **Customer is with family and engaged in discussion**: Allow them space but check in after 3–4 minutes.

The key principle: Be available without being intrusive. Warby Parker's retail model, widely studied for its customer experience (Forbes), emphasises that the best retail staff act as "helpful guides" rather than "pushy salespeople."

### 4.4 Farewell Protocol

The farewell is as important as the greeting. Every customer who leaves the store must receive a courteous send-off: "Thank you for visiting! Please come again." (Tamil: "Nandri, vaanga thirumba!"). If they made a purchase: "Your spectacles will be ready by [date/time]. We'll call you. Please don't hesitate to come back for any adjustments."

For customers who did not make a purchase: "No problem at all! You're welcome to visit anytime. We're here whenever you need us." This non-transactional warmth ensures the customer remembers the shop positively and returns when ready.

## 5. Eye Test Experience Enhancement

### 5.1 Pre-Test Preparation

The eye test is the most clinically significant part of the customer's visit and the foundation of their trust in our shop. A well-conducted eye test differentiates a professional optical shop from a frame-selling retail counter. Our pre-test process ensures efficiency and comfort:

- **Registration**: Collect the customer's name, age, contact number, and any relevant medical history (diabetes, hypertension, family history of glaucoma). This data is entered into the patient management system or a physical register.
- **Wait Time Management**: If the optometrist is occupied, inform the customer of the expected wait time honestly. Offer a seat in the waiting area with water and reading material. Do not over-promise — if the wait is 20 minutes, say 20 minutes, not "just 5 minutes."
- **Previous Prescription Review**: If the customer has previous spectacles or a prescription, these should be collected and reviewed by the optometrist before the test begins. This provides baseline data for comparison.
- **Pre-Test Anxiety Reduction**: Many customers, especially first-time visitors and elderly patients, feel anxious about eye tests. Staff should reassure them: "This is a simple and painless test. Our doctor will take good care of you."

### 5.2 During the Test

- **Comfortable Environment**: The eye test room must be at a comfortable temperature, well-ventilated, and free from noise. The phoropter or trial frame must be cleaned with an alcohol wipe before each use.
- **Patient Education**: The optometrist should explain each step of the test in simple language. "Now I'm going to ask you to read the letters on the chart. Tell me which lens makes it clearer — number 1 or number 2?" This involvement, a principle highlighted by RevolutionEHR's patient engagement research, empowers patients and improves satisfaction.
- **Adequate Time**: A comprehensive eye test must take a minimum of 20 minutes. Rushed tests lead to inaccurate prescriptions, which destroy customer trust. The optometrist should never compromise on test quality, even during peak hours.
- **Cover Test & Binocular Vision**: Beyond simple visual acuity, the optometrist should perform basic tests for binocular vision, colour vision (if relevant), and near-point accommodation (especially for customers above 40 years who may be developing presbyopia).
- **Eye Health Screening**: Where equipment permits, the optometrist should perform a basic eye health check (external eye examination, pupil response, and intraocular pressure measurement if a tonometer is available). Customers appreciate the added value of a health check beyond just a prescription.

### 5.3 Post-Test Communication

After the test, the optometrist should sit with the customer and explain the results clearly:
- **Current vision status**: "Your number has increased slightly compared to last year."
- **Specific conditions**: "You have early signs of presbyopia, which is common after age 40. You'll need reading glasses for near work."
- **Lens recommendation**: "Based on your prescription and lifestyle, I recommend progressive lenses because you use both distance and near vision throughout the day."
- **Eye health advice**: "Given that you have diabetes, I recommend an annual comprehensive eye check including retinal examination."

This post-test consultation is where clinical expertise meets customer care. A well-explained prescription builds confidence and justifies the lens recommendation that follows.

### 5.4 Prescription Handover

The customer must receive a written prescription (or a digital copy via WhatsApp) whether or not they purchase from our shop. This is both ethical practice and good business — customers who receive their prescription without obligation are far more likely to return and recommend the shop.

## 6. After-Sales Service Policy

### 6.1 The After-Sales Philosophy

In the optical retail industry, after-sales service is the single strongest driver of customer loyalty and word-of-mouth referrals. According to iCareLabs' research on optical customer retention, customers who receive proactive after-sales follow-up are 3x more likely to return for their next purchase and 2x more likely to recommend the shop to others. In a close-knit community like Sankarankovil, a single positive after-sales experience can generate 5–10 new customer referrals through family and social networks.

At Sankaran Kovil Opticals, after-sales service is not an afterthought — it is a planned, structured, and measured component of our customer journey.

### 6.2 Free Services Included with Every Purchase

The following services are provided free of charge to all customers who have purchased spectacles from our shop, for the lifetime of the frame:

- **Frame Adjustment & Tightening**: Nose pad adjustment, temple bend adjustment, screw tightening. No appointment needed — walk-in service.
- **Lens Cleaning**: Professional ultrasonic cleaning of lenses (if equipment available) or manual cleaning with lint-free cloth and approved cleaning solution.
- **Screw & Nose Pad Replacement**: Free replacement of standard screws and silicone nose pads.
- **Prescription Re-Verification**: If the customer feels the new spectacles are not comfortable within the first 30 days, we will re-check the prescription and verify the lens parameters against the original order at no charge.

### 6.3 Follow-Up Communication

- **Day 1–2 (Pick-up Call)**: When the customer's spectacles are ready, call them to inform and invite them for a fitting. Do not simply say "Come and collect." Say: "Your new spectacles are ready! Our team will ensure a perfect fit when you come in."
- **Day 7 (Comfort Check Call)**: Exactly one week after the customer collects their spectacles, a staff member should call to ask: "How are your new spectacles? Are you comfortable? Is there any dizziness or strain?" This proactive call catches adjustment issues early and demonstrates genuine care.
- **Day 30 (Satisfaction Check)**: A brief WhatsApp message or call at the 30-day mark: "It's been a month since you got your new spectacles. We hope you're enjoying clear vision! Please visit us anytime for free adjustments."
- **6 Months (Eye Health Reminder)**: A reminder for the next eye check: "It's time for your six-monthly eye check. Please visit us at your convenience. We look forward to seeing you!"

### 6.4 Warranty Policy

- **Frame Warranty**: 12 months manufacturing defect warranty on all frames. This covers hinge breakage, coating peeling, and structural failure under normal use. It does not cover accidental damage, sitting on the spectacles, or exposure to extreme heat.
- **Lens Warranty**: 12 months warranty on lens manufacturing defects (including delamination, coating defects, and incorrect prescription as per the ordered specifications).
- **Extended Warranty**: Customers who purchase frames above ₹2000 may be offered an optional extended warranty (additional 12 months) for a nominal fee.

### 6.5 Lifetime Relationship Building

Every customer should feel that Sankaran Kovil Opticals is their "family optician." Store their purchase history, prescription history, and preferences in the customer database. On their birthday, send a personalised greeting via WhatsApp with a small discount offer. During festival seasons, send seasonal greetings with relevant offers (e.g., "Gift your parents the gift of clear vision this Deepavali").

## 7. Return & Exchange Policy

### 7.1 Policy Statement

We understand that choosing the right spectacles is a significant and personal decision. Our return and exchange policy is designed to give customers confidence in their purchase while maintaining practical business sustainability. We follow the spirit of the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (India), which mandates fair treatment of consumers and transparent policies.

### 7.2 Frame Exchange (Within 7 Days)

- **Eligibility**: Frames may be exchanged within 7 days of purchase if the customer is not satisfied with the fit, style, or comfort — provided the frame is in original condition (no scratches, damage, or alterations).
- **Process**: The customer visits the store, selects an alternative frame, and pays any price difference. If the new frame is less expensive, the difference is refunded or credited to the customer's account for future use.
- **Lens Remounting**: If lenses have already been fitted, the cost of re-cutting/lens remounting on the new frame (if the lens size is compatible) will be borne by the shop. If the new frame requires new lenses, the customer pays only for the new lenses.
- **Limit**: One exchange per purchase. The exchanged frame cannot be further exchanged.

### 7.3 Lens Return (Within 14 Days)

- **Prescription Accuracy Guarantee**: If the lenses do not match the prescribed parameters (verified by an independent optometrist or our own re-check), we will remake the lenses at no charge within 14 days of delivery.
- **Adaptation Period**: Progressive lenses and high-prescription lenses require an adaptation period of 7–14 days. Customers experiencing initial discomfort will be counselled and given time to adapt. If after 14 days the customer is still uncomfortable, we will evaluate the prescription and remake if necessary.
- **Non-Returnable Items**: Custom-tinted lenses, polarised lenses (once tinted), and lenses with special coatings ordered specifically for the customer cannot be returned unless there is a manufacturing defect.

### 7.4 Refund Policy

- Full refunds will be processed within 7 working days for products returned due to manufacturing defects verified by the shop.
- For change-of-mind returns on frames (within 7 days, original condition), a refund will be issued minus a 10% restocking fee, or the full amount can be credited as store credit with no deduction.
- Refunds will be processed through the same payment method used for the original purchase (cash, UPI, card).
- A no-questions-asked refund is available within 48 hours for defective products. After that, the shop may request an evaluation before processing the refund.

### 7.5 Sunglass Return Policy

- Sunglasses (non-prescription) may be returned or exchanged within 7 days if in original condition with all tags intact.
- Prescription sunglasses follow the same policy as regular prescription spectacles.

### 7.6 Policy Display

The complete return and exchange policy must be clearly displayed at the billing counter, on the back of the purchase receipt, and on the shop's website/WhatsApp business profile. Customers must be verbally informed of the policy at the time of purchase.

## 8. Frame Adjustment & Repair Service

### 8.1 Service Philosophy

Frame adjustment and repair is one of the most underutilised yet powerful customer loyalty tools in optical retail. Many customers walk into an optical shop simply because their spectacles feel loose, a screw is missing, or a nose pad has fallen off. How the shop handles this "non-revenue" visit determines whether that customer returns for their next ₹5000 purchase. Our policy: **Every walk-in repair customer is a potential future buyer. Treat them with the same enthusiasm as a new customer.**

### 8.2 Walk-In Adjustment Service

- **Availability**: Frame adjustments are available during all working hours — no appointment needed.
- **Service Time**: Target service time is 10 minutes or less. If the shop is busy, acknowledge the customer and provide a realistic wait time.
- **Free for Our Customers**: All customers who have purchased from Sankaran Kovil Opticals receive free lifetime adjustments. This includes nose pad replacement, temple bend adjustment, bridge adjustment, and screw tightening.
- **Paid for Non-Customers**: For customers who did not purchase from us, a nominal fee of ₹20–50 is charged for adjustments and minor repairs. This fee is waived if the customer makes a purchase during the same visit.

### 8.3 Repair Services

| Repair Type | Description | Time | Charge (Our Customers) | Charge (Others) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Screw replacement | Replace lost or stripped frame screws | 5 min | Free | ₹20 |
| Nose pad replacement | Replace worn or broken silicone nose pads | 5 min | Free | ₹30 |
| Temple tip repair | Replace damaged temple end pieces | 10 min | Free | ₹50 |
| Hinge repair | Re-attach broken hinge (if repairable) | 30 min | ₹50 | ₹100 |
| Frame soldering | Re-join broken metal frame (outsourced) | 2–3 days | ₹100 | ₹200 |
| Lens remounting | Fit existing lenses into a new frame | 30 min | ₹100 | ₹200 |
| Temple replacement | Replace entire temple arm | 20 min | ₹50–100 | ₹100–200 |

### 8.4 Repair Quality Standards

- All repaired frames must pass a visual inspection and a comfort check (the customer should wear the repaired spectacles and confirm fit before leaving).
- Temporary fixes (e.g., tape, wire) are never acceptable. If a frame cannot be properly repaired, the customer must be informed honestly and offered a replacement frame option.
- Staff performing repairs must be trained in the use of optical tools (mini screwdrivers, optical pliers, heating pads for acetate adjustment) to avoid causing additional damage.

### 8.5 "While You Wait" Promise

For adjustments and minor repairs (screw replacement, nose pad replacement), we commit to a "while you wait" service. The customer can sit comfortably, have a glass of water, and their spectacles will be returned within 10 minutes. This turns a potentially frustrating experience (broken spectacles) into a positive brand interaction.

### 8.6 Repair Record Keeping

All repair services must be logged in the repair register with: customer name, phone number, date, type of repair, charges (if any), and staff member who performed the repair. This data helps identify recurring issues with specific frame brands and provides a record for warranty claims.

## 9. Customer Complaint Handling Process

### 9.1 Philosophy: Complaints Are Gifts

A customer who complains is a customer who cares enough to give us a chance to fix the problem. Research by the American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) shows that customers whose complaints are resolved effectively are more loyal than customers who never had a problem. Our complaint handling philosophy is rooted in empathy, speed, and fairness.

### 9.2 Complaint Categories

| Category | Examples | Severity Level |
|---|---|---|
| Product Quality | Incorrect prescription, lens defect, frame breakage, coating peeling | High |
| Service Quality | Rude staff, long wait times, rushed eye test, poor communication | Medium-High |
| Pricing & Billing | Overcharging, hidden charges, incorrect refund amount | High |
| After-Sales Issue | Delayed delivery, no follow-up call, repair not done properly | Medium |
| Store Environment | Dirty store, broken AC, poor lighting, uncomfortable seating | Low-Medium |
| Miscommunication | Wrong delivery date, wrong frame ordered, promise not kept | Medium |

### 9.3 Three-Level Escalation Framework

**Level 1 — Frontline Resolution (Immediate, within 15 minutes)**
- Handled by: Any senior staff member or floor manager
- Examples: Simple billing correction, frame adjustment, scheduling issue, minor communication misunderstanding
- Approach: LISTEN → APOLOGISE → FIX → THANK
- Documentation: Log in the complaint register

**Level 2 — Management Resolution (Within 48 hours)**
- Handled by: Store Manager / Owner
- Examples: Incorrect prescription dispute, refund request, repeated service failure, staff behaviour complaint
- Approach: Acknowledge within 2 hours → Investigate → Propose resolution → Get customer approval → Implement → Follow up
- Documentation: Formal complaint form with full details, investigation notes, resolution, and customer sign-off

**Level 3 — Escalation (Within 7 working days)**
- Handled by: Business Owner with external consultation (if needed — e.g., lens manufacturer)
- Examples: Major product defect requiring manufacturer involvement, legal threat, social media complaint, consumer forum complaint
- Approach: Immediate acknowledgment → Full investigation → Written response → Appropriate remedy (replacement, refund, compensation)
- Documentation: Complete file with all correspondence, evidence, and resolution

### 9.4 The LISTEN-ACT Framework

Every complaint, regardless of severity, follows the **LISTEN-ACT** framework:

1. **L**isten actively — Let the customer speak fully without interruption. Take notes.
2. **I**dentify the emotion — Acknowledge their frustration: "I understand this is very inconvenient for you."
3. **S**ummarise the issue — Repeat back what you understood: "So the issue is that your new spectacles are giving you headaches after two weeks of use."
4. **T**ake ownership — "I will personally make sure this is resolved." Do not deflect blame to the lab, the manufacturer, or another staff member.
5. **E**xplain the resolution — "Here is what I can do for you..."
6. **N**ote it down — Record every detail in the complaint register.
7. **A**ct immediately — Begin the resolution process right away.
8. **C**heck back — Follow up with the customer within 24 hours to confirm they are satisfied.

### 9.5 Complaint Register

A physical complaint register must be maintained at the billing counter. Every complaint, regardless of how minor, is recorded with:
- Date and time
- Customer name and phone number
- Nature of complaint
- Severity level
- Staff member handling
- Resolution provided
- Customer satisfaction with resolution (Yes/No)
- Follow-up date

The register is reviewed weekly by the store manager and monthly by the business owner. Trends are analysed to identify systemic issues.

### 9.6 Consumer Protection Act Compliance

In compliance with the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (India), the shop must:
- Display the grievance officer's name and contact at the store
- Provide a written acknowledgement of every complaint
- Resolve complaints within the timelines specified above
- Inform the customer of their right to approach the District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission if unsatisfied with the resolution

## 11. Service Recovery Strategies

### 11.1 The Cost of Poor Service Recovery

In a small town like Sankarankovil, a single unresolved service failure can reach hundreds of potential customers through word-of-mouth, temple gatherings, local WhatsApp groups, and social media. Conversely, a brilliantly handled service recovery can turn an angry customer into a vocal advocate. Research by the Harvard Business Review and customer service studies globally show that customers who experience excellent service recovery become 15–20% more loyal than customers who never had a problem.

### 11.2 The Service Recovery Paradox

The "Service Recovery Paradox" is a well-documented phenomenon: a customer who experiences a problem that is resolved exceptionally well often ends up more satisfied than a customer who never had a problem at all. This is because the recovery effort demonstrates that the business truly cares. At Sankaran Kovil Opticals, we aim to create these "recovery paradox" moments consistently.

### 11.3 Recovery Toolkit — What Staff Can Offer

| Situation | Recovery Action | Approval Level |
|---|---|---|
| Customer waited longer than promised | Sincere apology + ₹50 discount on current purchase | Floor staff |
| Minor billing error | Immediate correction + free lens cleaning kit | Floor staff |
| Incorrect frame delivered | Priority remake at no charge + free frame upgrade | Store Manager |
| Customer had to visit twice for same issue | Apology + free anti-reflective coating upgrade | Store Manager |
| Wrong prescription (shop's error) | Full remake + free sunglasses OR ₹500 store credit | Store Manager |
| Staff misbehaviour reported | Written apology from management + ₹200 store credit | Business Owner |
| Major quality issue (lens defect after 6 months) | Free replacement + 6-month warranty extension | Business Owner |
| Social media complaint | Immediate response + direct phone call + generous resolution | Business Owner |

### 11.4 Empowerment Principle

Staff must be empowered to resolve complaints up to ₹200 in value without seeking management approval. This eliminates the frustrating "let me check with my manager" delay that irritates customers. For recovery actions above ₹200, the store manager has authority up to ₹1000. Only the business owner can approve recovery actions above ₹1000.

### 11.5 The HEART Recovery Model

All service recovery interactions follow the **HEART** model:

1. **H**ear the customer out completely. Do not interrupt, argue, or become defensive.
2. **E**mpathise genuinely. Put yourself in the customer's shoes. "I would feel the same way in your position."
3. **A**pologise sincerely. A genuine apology is not an admission of legal liability — it is an acknowledgment of the customer's experience. "I am truly sorry for this inconvenience."
4. **R**esolve the issue quickly. Speed is critical. The longer a complaint drags on, the more frustrated the customer becomes. Provide a clear timeline: "I will have this resolved for you by [specific time/date]."
5. **T**hank the customer. "Thank you for bringing this to our attention. Your feedback helps us improve." This transforms a negative interaction into a positive one.

### 11.6 Follow-Up After Recovery

Within 48 hours of resolving a complaint, a staff member must call the customer to confirm satisfaction: "I just wanted to check — are you happy with how we resolved the issue? Is there anything else we can do?" This follow-up call is the final step in converting a dissatisfied customer into a loyal advocate.

## 13. Mystery Shopper Program

### 13.1 Purpose & Objectives

The Mystery Shopper Program is a critical quality assurance tool that provides objective, unbiased evaluation of the actual customer experience at Sankaran Kovil Opticals. Unlike customer feedback (which is self-selected and often biased towards very satisfied or very dissatisfied customers), mystery shopping captures the reality of day-to-day service delivery. According to BestMark's research on effective mystery shopping programs, the primary purpose is to improve specific employee behaviours — not to punish.

Our objectives are:
1. Measure compliance with customer service standards (greeting, assistance, eye test quality, farewell)
2. Evaluate staff product knowledge and communication skills
3. Assess store environment (cleanliness, lighting, display organisation, temperature)
4. Identify training gaps and coaching opportunities
5. Benchmark service quality month-over-month

### 13.2 Mystery Shopper Selection

Mystery shoppers must be individuals who are not regular customers of the shop and whose identity is unknown to the staff. Ideal candidates:
- Friends or family members of the business owner from a different town/city
- College students from nearby institutions (e.g., from Tirunelveli or Rajapalayam)
- Local professionals (teachers, bank employees) who can realistically pose as optical customers
- External mystery shopping agencies (if budget permits — approximately ₹2000–5000 per visit)

A pool of 4–6 mystery shoppers should be maintained to ensure staff cannot identify a pattern.

### 13.3 Mystery Shopper Visit Frequency

| Frequency | Type of Visit | Focus Area |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly (1 visit) | Full customer journey | Comprehensive evaluation of all service touchpoints |
| Quarterly (1 visit) | Complaint scenario | Test how staff handle a simulated complaint |
| Bi-annually (1 visit) | After-sales scenario | Test the repair/adjustment service experience |

### 13.4 Evaluation Checklist

The mystery shopper completes a detailed evaluation form (adapted from SafetyCulture and GoAudits retail mystery shopper templates) covering:

**Section A — Store Exterior & Entry (15 points)**
- Store signage visible and well-maintained
- Entrance clean and inviting
- Door opens/closes easily
- Window displays attractive and current

**Section B — Greeting & Needs Assessment (20 points)**
- Greeted within 30 seconds
- Greeting was warm and genuine
- Staff made eye contact and smiled
- Staff asked open-ended questions about needs
- Needs assessment was thorough (minimum 3 questions asked)

**Section C — Frame Selection Experience (20 points)**
- Staff guided the shopper to appropriate frame section
- Staff suggested frames based on face shape/lifestyle
- Staff explained frame materials and pricing clearly
- Shopper was allowed to try multiple frames without pressure
- Mirror and lighting were adequate for trying on frames

**Section D — Eye Test Experience (25 points)**
- Waiting area was clean and comfortable
- Wait time was communicated honestly
- Optometrist introduced themselves
- Test was thorough (minimum 15 minutes observed)
- Optometrist explained results in simple language
- Prescription was provided at the end

**Section E — Store Environment (10 points)**
- Store was clean and organised
- Lighting was adequate
- Temperature was comfortable
- Frame displays were well-organised and labelled with prices
- Music/noise level was comfortable

**Section F — Farewell & Overall Impression (10 points)**
- Staff thanked the shopper for visiting
- Farewell was warm and inviting
- Overall experience felt genuine (not scripted)
- Shopper would recommend the shop to a friend

**Total: 100 points**

### 13.5 Scoring Interpretation

| Score Range | Rating | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| 90–100 | Excellent | Recognise and reward the team |
| 75–89 | Good | Minor coaching on specific areas |
| 60–74 | Satisfactory | Targeted training on weak areas |
| Below 60 | Needs Improvement | Immediate management intervention, retraining |

### 13.6 Reporting & Follow-Up

- The mystery shopper submits the completed evaluation within 24 hours of the visit.
- The store manager reviews the report within 48 hours and shares key findings (anonymised) at the next team meeting.
- Individual staff coaching sessions are conducted privately for any area scoring below 70%.
- Mystery shopper scores are tracked monthly on a trend graph displayed in the staff room.
- After 6 months of consistent improvement, reward the team with a celebration (team lunch, outing, or bonus).

## 14. Customer Satisfaction Measurement

### 14.1 Measurement Framework

Measuring customer satisfaction is not a one-time activity — it is a continuous process that provides the data foundation for all service improvement initiatives. At Sankaran Kovil Opticals, we use a multi-dimensional measurement framework that combines quantitative metrics with qualitative insights. This approach is consistent with best practices recommended by MaximEyes and the Sightview patient satisfaction frameworks.

### 14.2 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)

| KPI | Definition | Target | Measurement Method | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Net Promoter Score (NPS) | % Promoters minus % Detractors | >60 | Post-visit survey | Monthly |
| Customer Satisfaction Score (CSAT) | Average rating (1–5 stars) | >4.5 | WhatsApp + In-store cards | Monthly |
| Customer Effort Score (CES) | "How easy was it to get what you needed?" | >4.0/5 | Post-visit survey | Monthly |
| Repeat Customer Rate | % of customers who have purchased 2+ times | >40% | CRM / Billing data | Quarterly |
| Referral Rate | % of new customers who cite personal referral | >30% | New customer registration form | Quarterly |
| Complaint Rate | # of complaints per 100 transactions | <3 | Complaint register | Monthly |
| Complaint Resolution Rate | % of complaints resolved within SLA | >95% | Complaint register | Monthly |
| First-Contact Resolution | % of complaints resolved at Level 1 | >70% | Complaint register | Monthly |
| Google Review Rating | Average Google star rating | >4.5 | Google Business Profile | Weekly |
| Walk-in to Purchase Conversion | % of walk-ins who make a purchase | >25% | POS data / Footfall estimate | Monthly |

### 14.3 Net Promoter Score (NPS) Deep Dive

NPS is our primary loyalty metric. After every purchase, we ask the customer: "On a scale of 0–10, how likely are you to recommend Sankaran Kovil Opticals to a friend or family member?"

- **Promoters (9–10)**: Loyal enthusiasts who will recommend us. Target: >60%
- **Passives (7–8)**: Satisfied but unenthusiastic. Vulnerable to competitors. Target: <30%
- **Detractors (0–6)**: Unhappy customers who may speak negatively. Target: <10%

NPS = % Promoters − % Detractors

We aim for an NPS of 60+, which places us among the top-performing optical retail stores nationally. According to iCareLabs, the average NPS for independent optical practices is approximately 45–55. Our goal is to exceed this benchmark.

### 14.4 Quarterly Customer Experience Report

Every quarter, the Customer Experience Manager (or store manager) prepares a comprehensive report that includes:

1. **NPS Trend** — Month-over-month NPS with trend line
2. **CSAT Breakdown** — Satisfaction scores by service touchpoint (greeting, eye test, frame selection, billing, farewell)
3. **Complaint Analysis** — Number, type, resolution rate, and trends
4. **Mystery Shopper Results** — Latest scores with comparison to previous quarter
5. **Repeat Purchase & Referral Data** — Loyalty metrics from billing data
6. **Google & Social Media Review Summary** — Sentiment analysis of online reviews
7. **Top 3 Wins** — What the team did well this quarter
8. **Top 3 Improvements** — What needs to change next quarter
9. **Action Plan** — Specific, measurable actions with deadlines and responsible persons

### 14.5 Customer Satisfaction Dashboard

A simple physical dashboard (whiteboard or printed poster) displayed in the staff room showing:
- Current month's NPS (large number, updated weekly)
- Current month's CSAT (large number, updated weekly)
- Number of days since last unresolved complaint
- This month's Google Review count and average rating
- Mystery Shopper score (latest visit)
- A "You said, We did" section: Show 2–3 examples of customer feedback and the action taken

This visual dashboard keeps customer satisfaction top-of-mind for the entire team.

### 14.6 Benchmarking

To contextualise our performance, we will benchmark against:
- **Industry benchmarks**: Average NPS for optical retail (45–55), average CSAT for retail healthcare (4.0–4.5)
- **Regional competition**: Periodically visit competitors' stores (Lawrence & Mayo in Tirunelveli, local optical shops in Sankarankovil) and compare service elements
- **Internal benchmarks**: Compare this month's metrics to the same month last year (seasonal adjustment) and to the previous month (trend analysis)

### 14.7 Continuous Improvement Cycle

The satisfaction measurement system feeds into a continuous improvement cycle using the **Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA)** methodology:

1. **Plan**: Based on customer feedback and satisfaction data, identify the most impactful improvement area
2. **Do**: Implement a specific change (e.g., reduce wait time by adding a second eye test slot)
3. **Check**: Measure the impact on satisfaction scores in the following month
4. **Act**: If successful, standardise the change. If not, adjust and try again.

This cycle ensures that customer satisfaction is not just measured but actively improved every month.
